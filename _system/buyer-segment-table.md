# Buyer Segment Table — EASCO

> Identifies who is landing on each page, what their primary pain is, what drives their decision,
> and how copy should be framed for them.
> Every Page Brief (Step 0) must name the primary buyer segment for that page.

---

## The Five Buyer Drivers (from Master Writing System)

| Driver | What it means for EASCO buyers |
|---|---|
| **Safety** | Will this system fail and create a security breach? Will staff get injured by a gate? |
| **Convenience** | Does it integrate with what we already have? Is installation straightforward? |
| **Status** | Will this make the facility look professional and modern to visitors and clients? |
| **Durability** | Will it still work in 3 years? What happens in extreme heat/cold/dust? |
| **ROI / Value** | Can I justify this cost? What does it replace? What does failure cost? |

---

## Segment 1 — Facility / Security Manager

**Who:** Head of security or facilities at a corporate office, hospital, university, or government building. Makes or influences the purchase decision. Answers to a senior director.

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Primary pain | A security incident happened, or they've been asked to upgrade. Justifying spend to finance. |
| Primary driver | Safety + Durability |
| What they read | Spec tables, IP ratings, throughput numbers, integration compatibility |
| What they fear | Buying the wrong system — one that fails, doesn't integrate, or embarrasses them |
| Messaging angle | "Here is the exact spec. Here is how it integrates. Here is what happens if it fails." |
| Key differentiators | D3 (multi-modal biometrics), D5 (liveness detection), D14 (anti-crush), D4 (iris accuracy) |
| Key objections | O5 (integration), O10 (outdoor), O11 (safety), O13 (spoofing), O16 (accuracy) |

**Pages they land on:** S1 pillar, S2 pillar, product pages, FAQ

---

## Segment 2 — System Integrator / Contractor

**Who:** Technical contractor who specifies and installs access control for multiple client projects. Buys regularly. Wants reliable supply, clean documentation, and SDK access.

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Primary pain | Clients want modern biometric systems but most suppliers can't supply consistent product + technical support |
| Primary driver | Convenience + Durability |
| What they read | SDK docs, API specs, Wiegand/RS485 compatibility, installation guides, datasheets |
| What they fear | Product that works in demo but fails on a live site. No support after sale. |
| Messaging angle | "Full documentation. Standard interfaces. We pick up when there's a problem." |
| Key differentiators | D2 (factory direct = direct support), D3 (full range), D10 (Android gate) |
| Key objections | O5 (integration), O3 (after-sale support), O8 (lead times), O12 (anti-tailgate) |

**Pages they land on:** SDK/API page, product spec pages, download center, FAQ

---

## Segment 3 — Importer / Distributor

**Who:** B2B buyer in Middle East, Southeast Asia, Africa or Europe importing hardware to resell or deploy for local clients. Buys in volume. Highly price-sensitive. Extremely supplier-risk-aware.

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Primary pain | Has been burned by a Chinese supplier before — late delivery, inconsistent quality, fake certs |
| Primary driver | ROI / Value + Durability |
| What they read | Company credentials, certifications, factory proof, pricing, MOQ, lead times |
| What they fear | Supplier disappears. Quality changes after first order. Certs fail customs. |
| Messaging angle | "20 years. Same factory. Same address. Documentation that clears customs." |
| Key differentiators | D1 (20yr), D2 (factory direct), D7 (Shenzhen supply chain) |
| Key objections | O1 (trading company), O2 (consistency), O3 (after-sale), O4 (certs), O6 (pricing), O7 (visit), O8 (lead times) |

**Pages they land on:** Homepage, About Us, Factory, Certifications, Partners page

---

## Segment 4 — OEM/ODM Buyer

**Who:** Company that wants to sell access control hardware under their own brand. May be a tech company, a systems house, or a regional security brand. Wants a manufacturing partner, not just a supplier.

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Primary pain | Wants branded hardware but doesn't have a factory. Previous OEM supplier delivered wrong spec. |
| Primary driver | Convenience + ROI |
| What they read | OEM capability statement, prototype process, MOQ, lead time, customisation scope |
| What they fear | Slow prototype. Factory pushes standard product. No firmware access. |
| Messaging angle | "Custom housing, firmware, branding. Prototype in 30–45 days. You own the spec." |
| Key differentiators | D6 (OEM speed), D2 (factory direct), D10 (Android gate — highly customisable) |
| Key objections | O19 (disappeared mid-project), O20 (will they match spec), O8 (lead times) |

**Pages they land on:** OEM/ODM page, Custom Solutions pillar, SDK/API, Contact

---

## Segment 5 — Airport / Transit Authority Procurement

**Who:** Procurement officer at an airport, metro authority, or government transport department. Large project, long procurement cycle, requires formal documentation and references.

| Field | Detail |
|---|---|
| Primary pain | Cannot find a single supplier who makes both the gates and the passenger-processing technology |
| Primary driver | Safety + Status |
| What they read | Case studies, certifications, product specifications, reference project list |
| What they fear | Supplier cannot deliver at required scale. Product fails in high-traffic environment. |
| Messaging angle | "Airport-grade hardware. From the gate to the kiosk. From one factory." |
| Key differentiators | D8 (airport solutions), D15 (24/7 display), D9 (0.5s gate speed), D1 (20yr) |
| Key objections | O1, O2, O4, O7, O8 |

**Pages they land on:** S5 Custom/Airport pillar, airport kiosk page, case studies, factory
