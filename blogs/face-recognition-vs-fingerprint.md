**H1: Face Recognition vs Fingerprint Access Control**

Face recognition verifies identity from a distance without physical contact. Fingerprint scanning requires touching a sensor and tends to fail on wet, dry, or worn fingertips. ### SECTION 2 — The Core Tradeoff: Contact vs. Contactless

**H2: Why the Two Technologies Solve Different Problems**

Fingerprint scanning has been the default biometric for decades because the hardware is cheap and the technology is well understood. It requires direct contact with a sensor, which creates two practical problems. A queue forms as each person places and lifts a finger. The sensor surface also accumulates oil, dirt, and wear that can degrade read accuracy over time.

Face recognition solves both problems by design. A camera captures the face from up to several meters away, so there's no physical contact and no queue caused by a slow sensor. The tradeoff moves to lighting conditions and camera quality — a low-quality camera in poor lighting reads less reliably than a well-maintained fingerprint sensor.

> Face recognition scans from a distance with no physical contact, typically completing in 200–300ms at over 99% accuracy on modern systems. Fingerprint scanning requires direct contact and can be affected by wet, dry, or worn fingertips, though it remains a lower-cost option for basic access control.

### SECTION 3 — Four Factors That Actually Decide the Choice

**H2: What to Weigh Before Picking One**

1. **Hygiene requirements:** A hospital, food processing facility, or high-traffic public entrance benefits from face recognition's no-contact operation.
2. **Environmental conditions:** Workers with consistently wet, dry, or worn fingertips (common in construction and manufacturing) get less reliable reads from fingerprint sensors. Face recognition avoids this failure mode entirely.
3. **Throughput needs:** A queue at a fingerprint sensor during a shift change costs real time. Face recognition's contactless scan generally clears a line faster.
4. **Liveness detection quality:** Both technologies can potentially be spoofed with a low-quality system — a printed fingerprint or a photo. Confirm the liveness detection tier regardless of which biometric is chosen.

### SECTION 6 — FAQ

**H2: Frequently Asked Questions**

**Q: Is face recognition more accurate than fingerprint scanning?**
A: Modern face recognition systems reach accuracy above 99%. Fingerprint accuracy varies more by sensor quality and finger condition — dirty, wet, or worn fingertips reduce reliability.

**Q: Why would a facility choose contactless biometrics over fingerprint?**
A: Contactless options like face recognition avoid the hygiene concerns and physical wear-and-tear that affect fingerprint sensors, and they don't create a bottleneck at a shared touch surface.

**Q: Can face recognition be fooled by a photo?**
A: Systems with financial-grade liveness detection reject photos, video replays, and printed masks. Confirm the liveness detection tier before assuming a system is spoof-resistant.
