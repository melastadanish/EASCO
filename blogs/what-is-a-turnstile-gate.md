### SECTION 1: Intro / Hook

**H1: What Is a Turnstile Gate?**

A turnstile gate is a physical barrier that allows one verified person to pass at a time, typically triggered by a card, biometric scan, or remote signal. Facility managers, system integrators, and anyone specifying access control for a lobby, campus, or transit point will find the core mechanics covered here. ### SECTION 2: What a Turnstile Gate Actually Does

**H2: One Person, One Scan, One Pass**

A turnstile gate mechanically enforces a rule that a card reader or camera alone cannot: only one person passes per valid credential. A rotating arm, a swinging flap panel, or a cylindrical rotating body physically blocks the passage until a valid scan releases it. The gate then re-locks automatically, either after a timed window or once a sensor confirms the person has passed through.

Most gates pair with a credential reader, a card, a face recognition terminal, or a QR code scanner, mounted on or beside the gate body. The gate itself doesn't decide who's authorized. It enforces the decision the credential system already made, physically preventing a second person from following through on one valid scan.

> A turnstile gate physically enforces one-person-per-scan access control, using a rotating arm, swinging flap, or cylindrical body, for facilities where a card reader alone can't stop tailgating. Gate speed ranges from roughly 30 to 50 people per minute depending on the mechanism, with anti-tailgating sensors flagging anyone following too closely behind an authorized pass.

### SECTION 3: Four Specs That Separate Good Systems from Bad Ones

**H2: What to Check Before Specifying Any Turnstile**

1. **Throughput (persons per minute):** A lobby handling a shift change needs a higher throughput rating than a low-traffic staff entrance. Confirm the number under normal operating conditions, not a theoretical maximum.
2. **Anti-tailgating detection:** Ask whether the gate uses an infrared sensor array to detect a second person following through, and how many sensor pairs it uses. More pairs generally means tighter detection.
3. **Anti-crush / anti-pinch mechanism:** Confirm whether the safety stop is mechanical (like a crank-link transmission with magnetic-sensitive limit control) or purely software-based. A mechanical stop keeps working even if the control software fails.
4. **Fail-safe behavior during a power outage:** Some gates release open on power loss for evacuation safety; others lock closed for security. Confirm which behavior the gate defaults to and whether it's configurable.

### SECTION 6: FAQ

**H2: Frequently Asked Questions**

**Q: What is a turnstile gate used for?**
A: Controlling entry so only one verified person passes per valid credential scan, commonly at office lobbies, transit stations, and campus entrances.

**Q: What's the difference between a tripod turnstile and a speed gate?**
A: A tripod turnstile uses a single rotating arm and suits lower-traffic entrances. A speed gate uses swinging panels and processes more people per minute, suited to higher-traffic lobbies.

**Q: Can a turnstile injure someone if it closes on them?**
A: A mechanical anti-crush mechanism, such as crank-link transmission with magnetic-sensitive limit control, stops the gate the instant it meets resistance, a safeguard independent of the control software.

**Q: How does a turnstile stop someone from following another person through?**
A: An infrared sensor array detects a second person passing behind an authorized scan and flags or blocks the attempt, depending on the gate's configuration.

**Q: Does a turnstile need a separate credential reader?**
A: Most gates pair with a card reader, face recognition terminal, or QR scanner mounted on or beside the gate. Some Android-integrated gates run the credential reader onboard, without a separate controller box.
