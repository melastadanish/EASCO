### SECTION 1: Intro / Hook

**H1: Biometric Access Control Explained**

Biometric access control verifies identity using a physical trait, a face, a palm vein pattern, or an iris. It replaces a card, key, or PIN that can be lost, copied, or shared. ### SECTION 2: How Biometric Access Control Actually Works

**H2: Matching a Live Scan Against an Enrolled Template**

A biometric system captures a physical trait during enrollment, a face, a vein pattern, or an iris. It stores that trait as a mathematical template, not a photo. Every subsequent scan gets compared against the stored template, and a match above a set confidence threshold grants access. The system never actually "recognizes" a person the way a human does. It calculates how closely two templates match and grants access once the match clears the threshold.

Two numbers describe how well a system performs this comparison. False acceptance rate (FAR) measures how often the system wrongly grants access to someone who shouldn't have it. False rejection rate (FRR) measures how often it wrongly denies someone who should have access. A lower FAR generally matters more for security. A lower FRR matters more for daily convenience.

> Biometric access control matches a live scan against an enrolled template, measured by false acceptance rate (FAR) and false rejection rate (FRR). Face recognition typically runs 99%+ accuracy, while specialized modes like iris recognition reach FAR figures as low as 1 in 10,000,000.

### SECTION 3: Four Specs That Separate Good Systems from Bad Ones

**H2: What to Check Before Specifying Any Biometric System**

1. **Liveness detection:** A system without liveness detection can potentially be fooled by a photo or video replay. Confirm whether the system is rated for financial-grade liveness detection or a lighter consumer-grade check.
2. **Published FAR/FRR figures:** A vendor should publish exact false acceptance and false rejection rates, not just a vague "highly accurate" claim.
3. **Recognition speed:** A system that takes several seconds per scan creates a queue during shift changes. Confirm the typical scan time under normal conditions.
4. **Data storage and privacy:** Some biometric modes, like palm vein. Don't store a facial image at all, relevant where facial data collection raises regulatory questions.

### SECTION 6: FAQ

**H2: Frequently Asked Questions**

**Q: What is biometric access control?**
A: A system that verifies identity using a physical trait, face, palm vein, or iris, matched against an enrolled template, instead of a card, key, or PIN.

**Q: What's the difference between FAR and FRR?**
A: FAR (false acceptance rate) measures how often the wrong person gets granted access. FRR (false rejection rate) measures how often the right person gets wrongly denied.

**Q: Can biometric access control be fooled by a photo?**
A: Systems with financial-grade liveness detection reject photos, video replays, and printed masks. Consumer-grade liveness detection may be easier to fool. Confirm the liveness detection tier before specifying.

**Q: Which biometric mode is most private?**
A: Palm vein recognition reads a subsurface vein pattern rather than a facial image, avoiding facial data storage for sites with privacy or regulatory concerns.

**Q: Which biometric mode is most accurate?**
A: Iris recognition typically reaches the lowest false acceptance rates among common biometric modes, as low as 1 in 10,000,000 on some systems.
